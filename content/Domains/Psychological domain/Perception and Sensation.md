## Perception and Sensation

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the processes through which sensory information is received, interpreted, and organized into meaningful experience. It captures how humans conceptualize the interface between their consciousness and the external world through the various sensory modalities and perceptual processes.

### Justification

Perception and sensation represent the fundamental interface between consciousness and reality. Humans naturally understand their sensory experiences as both subjective and informative about the external world. This subdomain is essential because it represents the primary means through which humans acquire information about their environment and the cognitive processes that transform raw sensory data into meaningful experience.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Sensory modalities** (vision, hearing, touch, taste, smell, proprioception, balance)
- **Perceptual qualities** (brightness, color, loudness, pitch, texture, temperature, flavor)
- **Spatial perception** (depth, distance, size, shape, orientation, movement detection)
- **Temporal perception** (duration, rhythm, sequence, simultaneity, change detection)
- **Perceptual organization** (figure-ground, pattern recognition, gestalt principles, grouping)
- **Sensory thresholds** (detection limits, discrimination abilities, sensitivity variations)
- **Perceptual illusions** (optical illusions, auditory illusions, misperceptions, hallucinations)
- **Cross-modal perception** (synesthesia, sensory substitution, multisensory integration)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own perceptual capabilities and limitations. They recognize the difference between sensation and perception, understand how context affects interpretation, and can metacognitively monitor their perceptual experiences.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Attention and Awareness" (which focuses on selective aspects of consciousness rather than sensory input) and "Subjective Experience of the Body and Environment" (which emphasizes felt experience rather than perceptual processing).
