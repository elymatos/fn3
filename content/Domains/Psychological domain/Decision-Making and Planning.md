## Decision-Making and Planning

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of choice processes, future-oriented thinking, and the various mechanisms through which humans select among alternatives and organize actions toward future goals. It encompasses both immediate decision-making and long-term strategic planning.

### Justification

Decision-making and planning are fundamental to human agency and goal achievement. Humans naturally understand choice as involving tradeoffs and planning as requiring foresight and organization. This subdomain is essential because it represents the prospective aspects of cognition that enable humans to act strategically and shape their future circumstances through deliberate choice and preparation.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Choice processes** (option generation, alternative evaluation, preference formation, commitment)
- **Planning activities** (goal setting, strategy formation, resource allocation, timeline development)
- **Decision factors** (costs and benefits, risks and rewards, uncertainty, value tradeoffs)
- **Temporal considerations** (short-term vs. long-term thinking, delayed gratification, future discounting)
- **Strategic thinking** (anticipating consequences, considering multiple scenarios, contingency planning)
- **Decision aids** (decision trees, pros and cons lists, advice seeking, deliberation strategies)
- **Planning tools** (schedules, priorities, milestones, resource management, coordination)
- **Choice difficulties** (decision paralysis, analysis paralysis, regret, satisficing vs. optimizing)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own decision-making processes and can use various strategies to improve choice quality. They recognize when decisions are difficult and can adapt their decision-making approach to the situation.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Reasoning and Problem-Solving" (which focuses on logical analysis rather than choice and action) and "Intention and Goal-Oriented Action" (which emphasizes goal formation rather than planning processes).
