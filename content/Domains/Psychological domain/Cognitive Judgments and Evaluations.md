## Cognitive Judgments and Evaluations

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of assessment processes, evaluative thinking, and the various ways humans form judgments about quality, value, truth, and significance. It captures how humans conceptualize evaluation, criticism, and the processes through which they assess and compare different options, ideas, or situations.

### Justification

Evaluative thinking is fundamental to human decision-making and social interaction. Humans naturally make judgments about quality, value, and appropriateness in all domains of life. This subdomain is essential because it represents the assessment dimension of cognition that enables humans to distinguish better from worse, appropriate from inappropriate, and valuable from worthless across various domains.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Quality assessments** (good vs. bad, excellent vs. poor, superior vs. inferior, adequate vs. inadequate)
- **Value judgments** (important vs. trivial, worthwhile vs. worthless, significant vs. insignificant)
- **Truth evaluations** (true vs. false, accurate vs. inaccurate, valid vs. invalid, credible vs. dubious)
- **Aesthetic judgments** (beautiful vs. ugly, elegant vs. crude, artistic vs. artless, tasteful vs. tasteless)
- **Comparative assessments** (better vs. worse, more vs. less, superior vs. inferior, preferable vs. undesirable)
- **Appropriateness judgments** (suitable vs. unsuitable, proper vs. improper, fitting vs. unfitting)
- **Evaluation criteria** (standards, benchmarks, expectations, requirements, ideals)
- **Judgment processes** (critical thinking, assessment strategies, evaluation frameworks, bias recognition)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated evaluation abilities and can apply various criteria to assess quality and value. They understand the difference between objective and subjective judgments and can recognize when their evaluations might be biased or incomplete.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Reasoning and Problem-Solving" (which focuses on logical analysis rather than evaluative assessment) and "Moral Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes ethical rather than general evaluative thinking).
