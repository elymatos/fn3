## Attention and Awareness

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of consciousness, selective attention, and the various states and processes through which humans become aware of and focus on particular aspects of their internal or external environment. It captures how humans conceptualize the dynamic, selective nature of conscious awareness and attentional control.

### Justification

Attention and awareness represent the fundamental gatekeeping mechanisms of consciousness. Humans naturally understand attention as a limited resource that can be directed and controlled. This subdomain is essential because it represents the executive aspects of consciousness that determine what enters awareness, how deeply it is processed, and how conscious resources are allocated across competing demands.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Attentional focus** (concentration, selective attention, divided attention, sustained attention)
- **Awareness states** (consciousness, mindfulness, alertness, vigilance, absent-mindedness)
- **Attentional control** (focusing, shifting attention, filtering distractions, mental effort)
- **Levels of awareness** (conscious, preconscious, unconscious, subliminal, peripheral awareness)
- **Attentional scope** (narrow focus, broad awareness, tunnel vision, global attention)
- **Meta-awareness** (awareness of awareness, attention to attention, metacognitive monitoring)
- **Altered states** (meditation, flow states, daydreaming, hypnosis, absorption)
- **Attentional disorders** (distractibility, hypervigilance, attention deficits, obsessive focus)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own attentional capabilities and can strategically direct attention to optimize performance. They recognize when attention is captured versus voluntarily directed and understand the relationship between attention and consciousness.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Perception and Sensation" (which focuses on sensory input rather than attentional selection) and "Memory and Learning" (which emphasizes information storage rather than conscious access).
