## Economic and Commercial Practices

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of exchange, trade, and economic relationships that involve the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services. It captures how humans conceptualize value, transaction, and the various forms of economic interaction in their social environment.

### Justification

Economic thinking is fundamental to human survival and cooperation. Humans naturally engage in reciprocal exchange and understand concepts of value, fairness, and economic obligation. This subdomain is essential because it represents a distinct domain of social interaction organized around resource allocation, trade, and economic relationships that shape much of social life.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Exchange mechanisms** (buying, selling, trading, bartering, gifting, sharing)
- **Financial concepts** (money, credit, debt, investment, savings, insurance)
- **Market activities** (shopping, negotiating, advertising, competing, pricing)
- **Work and labor** (employment, wages, contracts, business ownership, entrepreneurship)
- **Economic relationships** (customer-vendor, employer-employee, landlord-tenant)
- **Value concepts** (cost, profit, loss, worth, affordability, luxury vs. necessity)
- **Economic institutions** (banking, taxation, property rights, market regulation)
- **Resource management** (budgeting, planning, allocation, conservation, waste)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop intuitive understanding of economic value, fairness in exchange, and reciprocity obligations. They form mental models about market dynamics, resource scarcity, and economic opportunity that guide financial decision-making.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Institutions and Organizations" (which focuses on structural rather than transactional aspects) and "Governance and Law" (which emphasizes regulation rather than market activities).
