## Beliefs and Worldviews

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of fundamental perspectives on reality, existence, and meaning that shape how individuals and groups interpret their world. It captures the deep cognitive frameworks, belief systems, and philosophical orientations that guide human understanding of life's fundamental questions and provide interpretive lenses for experience.

### Justification

Worldviews and belief systems are fundamental to human cognition and social organization. Humans naturally form comprehensive frameworks for understanding reality that go beyond immediate empirical experience. This subdomain is essential because it represents the foundational cognitive structures that influence all other aspects of cultural life—how humans make sense of existence, morality, causation, and their place in the universe.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Religious beliefs** (monotheism, polytheism, animism, atheism, agnosticism, spirituality)
- **Cosmological views** (creation stories, afterlife concepts, divine intervention, fate vs. free will)
- **Philosophical orientations** (materialism, idealism, existentialism, determinism, humanism)
- **Explanatory frameworks** (scientific rationalism, mysticism, traditionalism, supernaturalism)
- **Value systems** (individualism vs. collectivism, progress vs. tradition, sacred vs. secular)
- **Meaning-making concepts** (purpose of life, suffering, justice, truth, beauty, goodness)
- **Epistemological beliefs** (how knowledge is acquired, sources of authority, truth criteria)
- **Metaphysical concepts** (nature of reality, consciousness, time, causation, unity vs. diversity)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop coherent worldview frameworks that provide cognitive scaffolding for interpreting experiences and making decisions. These belief systems offer psychological security, social identity, and behavioral guidance in uncertain situations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Moral" domain concepts (which focus on ethical evaluation rather than fundamental reality interpretation) and "Knowledge Representation" (which emphasizes information structure rather than belief content).
