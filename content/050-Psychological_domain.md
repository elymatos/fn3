# Psychological Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Perception and Sensation

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the processes through which sensory information is received, interpreted, and organized into meaningful experience. It captures how humans conceptualize the interface between their consciousness and the external world through the various sensory modalities and perceptual processes.

### Justification

Perception and sensation represent the fundamental interface between consciousness and reality. Humans naturally understand their sensory experiences as both subjective and informative about the external world. This subdomain is essential because it represents the primary means through which humans acquire information about their environment and the cognitive processes that transform raw sensory data into meaningful experience.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Sensory modalities** (vision, hearing, touch, taste, smell, proprioception, balance)
- **Perceptual qualities** (brightness, color, loudness, pitch, texture, temperature, flavor)
- **Spatial perception** (depth, distance, size, shape, orientation, movement detection)
- **Temporal perception** (duration, rhythm, sequence, simultaneity, change detection)
- **Perceptual organization** (figure-ground, pattern recognition, gestalt principles, grouping)
- **Sensory thresholds** (detection limits, discrimination abilities, sensitivity variations)
- **Perceptual illusions** (optical illusions, auditory illusions, misperceptions, hallucinations)
- **Cross-modal perception** (synesthesia, sensory substitution, multisensory integration)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own perceptual capabilities and limitations. They recognize the difference between sensation and perception, understand how context affects interpretation, and can metacognitively monitor their perceptual experiences.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Attention and Awareness" (which focuses on selective aspects of consciousness rather than sensory input) and "Subjective Experience of the Body and Environment" (which emphasizes felt experience rather than perceptual processing).

---

## 2. Emotions and Affective States

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of feeling states, emotional responses, and the complex affective experiences that color human consciousness. It encompasses how humans recognize, categorize, and experience the full spectrum of emotions and moods that influence cognition, behavior, and social interaction.

### Justification

Emotional experience is fundamental to human psychology and guides most significant decisions and behaviors. Humans naturally recognize and categorize emotional states in themselves and others. This subdomain is essential because it represents a distinct mode of psychological experience that integrates physiological arousal, subjective feeling, cognitive appraisal, and behavioral tendency into unified affective states.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Basic emotions** (joy, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, surprise, contempt, love)
- **Complex emotions** (guilt, shame, pride, envy, gratitude, nostalgia, awe, empathy)
- **Mood states** (depression, elation, irritability, contentment, anxiety, excitement)
- **Emotional intensity** (mild feelings, intense passion, overwhelming emotion, emotional numbness)
- **Affective qualities** (pleasant vs. unpleasant, arousing vs. calming, approach vs. avoidance)
- **Emotional regulation** (control, expression, suppression, amplification, coping strategies)
- **Social emotions** (embarrassment, jealousy, compassion, moral outrage, social anxiety)
- **Emotional development** (emotional maturity, emotional intelligence, feeling recognition)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated emotional awareness and regulation abilities. They understand emotional causation, recognize emotional expressions in others, and use emotional information to guide decision-making and social behavior.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Needs and Drives and Motivations" (which focuses on goal-directed forces rather than feeling states) and "Moral Emotions and Responses" (which emphasizes ethical rather than general emotional experience).

---

## 3. Needs and Drives and Motivations

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the internal forces that energize, direct, and sustain behavior toward goals. It captures how humans conceptualize the various biological and psychological needs, drives, and motivational states that influence action, decision-making, and goal pursuit.

### Justification

Motivational understanding is fundamental to human psychology and self-regulation. Humans naturally recognize their own needs and drives and understand motivation as the force behind behavior. This subdomain is essential because it represents the dynamic, goal-directed aspects of psychology that explain why humans act, what they seek to achieve, and how they maintain persistent effort toward objectives.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Biological needs** (hunger, thirst, sleep, sexual desire, pain avoidance, homeostatic drives)
- **Psychological needs** (autonomy, competence, relatedness, security, achievement, recognition)
- **Social motivations** (belonging, status, approval, intimacy, power, influence)
- **Growth motivations** (self-actualization, learning, creativity, meaning-seeking, spiritual fulfillment)
- **Motivational states** (desire, craving, ambition, passion, determination, apathy)
- **Goal orientations** (approach vs. avoidance, intrinsic vs. extrinsic, short-term vs. long-term)
- **Drive regulation** (impulse control, delayed gratification, habit formation, addiction)
- **Motivational conflicts** (competing desires, approach-avoidance conflicts, value conflicts)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of their own motivational patterns and can identify what drives their behavior. They recognize the hierarchy of needs, understand motivational conflict, and develop strategies for goal achievement and drive regulation.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Emotions and Affective States" (which focuses on feeling rather than driving forces) and "Intention and Goal-Oriented Action" (which emphasizes cognitive planning rather than motivational energy).

---

## 4. Subjective Experience of the Body and Environment

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the felt, phenomenological aspects of embodied existence and environmental experience. It encompasses how humans conceptualize their immediate, first-person experience of being a conscious body situated in a physical and social environment.

### Justification

Subjective bodily and environmental experience represents the foundation of human consciousness and self-awareness. Humans naturally attend to and interpret their felt sense of embodiment and environmental presence. This subdomain is essential because it represents the qualitative, experiential aspects of psychology that cannot be reduced to cognitive processing or behavioral responses—the "what it's like" dimension of human experience.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Bodily sensations** (comfort, discomfort, tension, relaxation, energy, fatigue, vitality)
- **Interoceptive awareness** (heartbeat, breathing, hunger, thirst, internal bodily signals)
- **Spatial embodiment** (body schema, personal space, embodied presence, spatial orientation)
- **Environmental presence** (sense of place, atmosphere, environmental mood, spatial qualities)
- **Phenomenological states** (flow states, altered consciousness, peak experiences, mindfulness)
- **Somatic responses** (gut feelings, embodied intuition, physical reactions to emotion)
- **Temporal embodiment** (sense of duration, temporal flow, present moment awareness)
- **Relational embodiment** (interpersonal presence, social attunement, embodied empathy)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated awareness of their subjective experience and can attend to and describe qualitative aspects of consciousness. They understand the relationship between mental states and bodily experience and use somatic information for decision-making.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Perception and Sensation" (which focuses on information processing rather than felt experience) and "Body and Anatomy" (which emphasizes structural rather than experiential aspects of embodiment).

---

## Summary

The Psychological domain captures the fundamental aspects of individual mental life and subjective experience. These four subdomains progress from the interface with external reality (Perception and Sensation), through the emotional coloring of experience (Emotions and Affective States), to the forces that drive behavior (Needs and Drives and Motivations), and finally to the qualitative, felt dimension of conscious experience (Subjective Experience of the Body and Environment). Together, they represent the core components of human psychological life as experienced from the first-person perspective.

# Cognitive Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Attention and Awareness

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of consciousness, selective attention, and the various states and processes through which humans become aware of and focus on particular aspects of their internal or external environment. It captures how humans conceptualize the dynamic, selective nature of conscious awareness and attentional control.

### Justification

Attention and awareness represent the fundamental gatekeeping mechanisms of consciousness. Humans naturally understand attention as a limited resource that can be directed and controlled. This subdomain is essential because it represents the executive aspects of consciousness that determine what enters awareness, how deeply it is processed, and how conscious resources are allocated across competing demands.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Attentional focus** (concentration, selective attention, divided attention, sustained attention)
- **Awareness states** (consciousness, mindfulness, alertness, vigilance, absent-mindedness)
- **Attentional control** (focusing, shifting attention, filtering distractions, mental effort)
- **Levels of awareness** (conscious, preconscious, unconscious, subliminal, peripheral awareness)
- **Attentional scope** (narrow focus, broad awareness, tunnel vision, global attention)
- **Meta-awareness** (awareness of awareness, attention to attention, metacognitive monitoring)
- **Altered states** (meditation, flow states, daydreaming, hypnosis, absorption)
- **Attentional disorders** (distractibility, hypervigilance, attention deficits, obsessive focus)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own attentional capabilities and can strategically direct attention to optimize performance. They recognize when attention is captured versus voluntarily directed and understand the relationship between attention and consciousness.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Perception and Sensation" (which focuses on sensory input rather than attentional selection) and "Memory and Learning" (which emphasizes information storage rather than conscious access).

---

## 2. Memory and Learning

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of how information is acquired, stored, retrieved, and modified over time. It encompasses the various forms of memory and learning processes through which humans accumulate knowledge, develop skills, and benefit from experience to guide future behavior and understanding.

### Justification

Memory and learning are fundamental to human adaptation and cultural transmission. Humans naturally understand memory as both reliable and fallible, and recognize learning as the process through which experience changes future behavior. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans build upon past experience to navigate current situations and plan for the future.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Memory types** (episodic, semantic, procedural, working memory, long-term memory, short-term memory)
- **Learning processes** (acquisition, practice, rehearsal, consolidation, skill development)
- **Memory phenomena** (forgetting, remembering, recognition, recall, false memories, flashbulb memories)
- **Learning strategies** (repetition, association, elaboration, organization, retrieval practice)
- **Memory cues** (reminders, associations, context, priming, retrieval cues)
- **Knowledge organization** (schemas, categories, mental models, conceptual frameworks)
- **Expertise development** (novice to expert progression, automaticity, domain knowledge)
- **Memory failures** (amnesia, confusion, tip-of-tongue, interference, decay)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own memory capabilities and learning processes. They use metacognitive strategies to optimize learning and can distinguish between different types of memory experiences and learning situations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Beliefs and Knowledge and Assumptions" (which focuses on content rather than processes) and "Knowledge Representation" (which emphasizes information structure rather than memory mechanisms).

---

## 3. Reasoning and Problem-Solving

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of logical thinking, analytical processes, and the systematic approaches used to work through complex problems and arrive at conclusions. It captures how humans conceptualize rational thought, inference-making, and the various strategies for overcoming cognitive challenges.

### Justification

Reasoning and problem-solving are fundamental to human intelligence and adaptation. Humans naturally engage in logical thinking and understand the difference between good and poor reasoning. This subdomain is essential because it represents the systematic, analytical aspects of cognition that enable humans to move beyond immediate experience to understand abstract relationships and solve novel problems.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Logical reasoning** (deduction, induction, abduction, syllogistic reasoning, logical validity)
- **Problem-solving strategies** (trial and error, means-ends analysis, working backward, analogical reasoning)
- **Analytical thinking** (breaking down problems, identifying patterns, systematic analysis)
- **Creative problem-solving** (brainstorming, lateral thinking, insight, innovative solutions)
- **Reasoning errors** (logical fallacies, cognitive biases, faulty assumptions, circular reasoning)
- **Evidence evaluation** (weighing evidence, assessing credibility, identifying relevant information)
- **Abstract thinking** (conceptual reasoning, hypothetical thinking, counterfactual reasoning)
- **Mathematical reasoning** (quantitative thinking, mathematical proof, statistical reasoning, numerical relationships)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of logical principles and can evaluate the quality of their own and others' reasoning. They recognize when problems require systematic versus intuitive approaches and can apply various problem-solving strategies.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Decision-Making and Planning" (which focuses on choice and future action rather than logical analysis) and "Cognitive Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes assessment rather than systematic reasoning).

---

## 4. Decision-Making and Planning

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of choice processes, future-oriented thinking, and the various mechanisms through which humans select among alternatives and organize actions toward future goals. It encompasses both immediate decision-making and long-term strategic planning.

### Justification

Decision-making and planning are fundamental to human agency and goal achievement. Humans naturally understand choice as involving tradeoffs and planning as requiring foresight and organization. This subdomain is essential because it represents the prospective aspects of cognition that enable humans to act strategically and shape their future circumstances through deliberate choice and preparation.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Choice processes** (option generation, alternative evaluation, preference formation, commitment)
- **Planning activities** (goal setting, strategy formation, resource allocation, timeline development)
- **Decision factors** (costs and benefits, risks and rewards, uncertainty, value tradeoffs)
- **Temporal considerations** (short-term vs. long-term thinking, delayed gratification, future discounting)
- **Strategic thinking** (anticipating consequences, considering multiple scenarios, contingency planning)
- **Decision aids** (decision trees, pros and cons lists, advice seeking, deliberation strategies)
- **Planning tools** (schedules, priorities, milestones, resource management, coordination)
- **Choice difficulties** (decision paralysis, analysis paralysis, regret, satisficing vs. optimizing)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own decision-making processes and can use various strategies to improve choice quality. They recognize when decisions are difficult and can adapt their decision-making approach to the situation.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Reasoning and Problem-Solving" (which focuses on logical analysis rather than choice and action) and "Intention and Goal-Oriented Action" (which emphasizes goal formation rather than planning processes).

---

## 5. Beliefs and Knowledge and Assumptions

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the information, beliefs, and assumptions that constitute their knowledge base and worldview. It captures how humans conceptualize what they know, how they know it, and the various forms of knowledge and belief that guide their understanding and behavior.

### Justification

Knowledge and belief systems are fundamental to human cognition and behavior. Humans naturally distinguish between what they know with certainty versus what they believe or assume. This subdomain is essential because it represents the cognitive content that shapes all other mental processes—the background knowledge and beliefs that provide context for perception, reasoning, and action.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Knowledge types** (factual knowledge, procedural knowledge, experiential knowledge, tacit knowledge)
- **Belief systems** (core beliefs, peripheral beliefs, religious beliefs, political beliefs, scientific beliefs)
- **Epistemic states** (certainty, doubt, confidence, skepticism, conviction, open-mindedness)
- **Knowledge sources** (experience, education, authority, intuition, tradition, evidence)
- **Assumptions** (background assumptions, default beliefs, implicit knowledge, taken-for-granted truths)
- **Belief revision** (learning, updating beliefs, changing minds, paradigm shifts, cognitive dissonance)
- **Knowledge organization** (mental models, frameworks, theories, conceptual structures)
- **Epistemic virtues** (intellectual humility, curiosity, critical thinking, open inquiry)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop metacognitive awareness of their own knowledge and beliefs and can distinguish between different levels of certainty and types of knowledge. They understand how beliefs influence perception and can engage in belief revision when confronted with new evidence.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Memory and Learning" (which focuses on acquisition processes rather than content) and "Beliefs and Worldviews" in the Cultural domain (which emphasizes shared cultural frameworks rather than individual cognitive content).

---

## 6. Intention and Goal-Oriented Action

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of purposive behavior, goal formation, and the various processes through which humans translate desires and intentions into organized action. It encompasses how humans conceptualize agency, willpower, and the relationship between mental intentions and behavioral execution.

### Justification

Intentional action is fundamental to human agency and self-determination. Humans naturally understand behavior as goal-directed and recognize the difference between intentional and accidental actions. This subdomain is essential because it represents the volitional aspects of cognition that enable humans to act deliberately and persist toward objectives despite obstacles and competing demands.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Goal formation** (desire, intention, commitment, goal setting, aspiration, ambition)
- **Action planning** (implementation intentions, action sequences, means-ends chains, strategy selection)
- **Volitional control** (willpower, self-control, effort, persistence, resistance to temptation)
- **Goal management** (goal pursuit, goal switching, goal abandonment, prioritization)
- **Intentional states** (trying, striving, attempting, endeavoring, working toward)
- **Action monitoring** (progress tracking, performance evaluation, course correction, adaptation)
- **Goal conflicts** (competing goals, approach-avoidance conflicts, value conflicts, resource allocation)
- **Agency experiences** (sense of control, self-efficacy, personal causation, empowerment)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of their own agency and can set, pursue, and modify goals strategically. They understand the relationship between intention and action and can use various strategies to maintain goal pursuit despite difficulties.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Decision-Making and Planning" (which focuses on choice processes rather than goal pursuit) and "Needs and Drives and Motivations" (which emphasizes motivational energy rather than intentional direction).

---

## 7. Cognitive Judgments and Evaluations

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of assessment processes, evaluative thinking, and the various ways humans form judgments about quality, value, truth, and significance. It captures how humans conceptualize evaluation, criticism, and the processes through which they assess and compare different options, ideas, or situations.

### Justification

Evaluative thinking is fundamental to human decision-making and social interaction. Humans naturally make judgments about quality, value, and appropriateness in all domains of life. This subdomain is essential because it represents the assessment dimension of cognition that enables humans to distinguish better from worse, appropriate from inappropriate, and valuable from worthless across various domains.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Quality assessments** (good vs. bad, excellent vs. poor, superior vs. inferior, adequate vs. inadequate)
- **Value judgments** (important vs. trivial, worthwhile vs. worthless, significant vs. insignificant)
- **Truth evaluations** (true vs. false, accurate vs. inaccurate, valid vs. invalid, credible vs. dubious)
- **Aesthetic judgments** (beautiful vs. ugly, elegant vs. crude, artistic vs. artless, tasteful vs. tasteless)
- **Comparative assessments** (better vs. worse, more vs. less, superior vs. inferior, preferable vs. undesirable)
- **Appropriateness judgments** (suitable vs. unsuitable, proper vs. improper, fitting vs. unfitting)
- **Evaluation criteria** (standards, benchmarks, expectations, requirements, ideals)
- **Judgment processes** (critical thinking, assessment strategies, evaluation frameworks, bias recognition)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated evaluation abilities and can apply various criteria to assess quality and value. They understand the difference between objective and subjective judgments and can recognize when their evaluations might be biased or incomplete.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Reasoning and Problem-Solving" (which focuses on logical analysis rather than evaluative assessment) and "Moral Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes ethical rather than general evaluative thinking).

---

## Summary

The Cognitive domain captures the fundamental mental processes and operations that constitute human thinking and rational cognition. These seven subdomains progress from the gatekeeping function of consciousness (Attention and Awareness), through information processing (Memory and Learning), systematic thinking (Reasoning and Problem-Solving), prospective cognition (Decision-Making and Planning), cognitive content (Beliefs and Knowledge and Assumptions), volitional control (Intention and Goal-Oriented Action), and evaluative thinking (Cognitive Judgments and Evaluations). Together, they represent the core operational components of human rational cognition and mental life.
