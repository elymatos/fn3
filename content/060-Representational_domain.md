# Representational Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Signs and Communication and Language

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of symbolic systems, communicative processes, and the various means through which humans create, transmit, and interpret meaningful information. It captures how humans conceptualize language, signs, symbols, and the full spectrum of communicative acts that enable shared understanding and cultural transmission.

### Justification

Communication through symbolic representation is uniquely human and fundamental to all cultural activity. Humans naturally understand the relationship between symbols and meanings and can use various communicative modalities. This subdomain is essential because it represents the primary mechanism through which humans share mental content, coordinate action, and transmit culture across individuals and generations.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Language systems** (spoken language, written language, sign language, grammar, vocabulary, syntax)
- **Symbolic communication** (gestures, facial expressions, body language, pictograms, icons, symbols)
- **Communicative acts** (speaking, listening, reading, writing, signing, storytelling, explaining)
- **Meaning-making** (interpretation, understanding, translation, disambiguation, context)
- **Communication channels** (verbal, nonverbal, visual, auditory, tactile, digital)
- **Discourse types** (conversation, narrative, argument, description, instruction, poetry)
- **Pragmatic dimensions** (tone, intent, context, audience, appropriateness, effectiveness)
- **Communication problems** (misunderstanding, ambiguity, language barriers, communication breakdown)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated understanding of communicative competence and can adapt their communication style to different contexts and audiences. They understand the relationship between form and meaning and can use metalinguistic awareness to reflect on language use.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Arts and Creative Practices" (which emphasizes aesthetic rather than communicative function) and "Information Structures" (which focuses on organization rather than communicative transmission).

## 2. Information Structures

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of how information is organized, categorized, and structured to enable efficient storage, retrieval, and manipulation. It encompasses the various ways humans conceptualize the arrangement and relationship of information elements, from simple lists to complex hierarchical and network structures.

### Justification

Information organization is fundamental to human cognition and knowledge management. Humans naturally create structures to organize complex information and understand relationships between information elements. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans impose order on information to make it manageable, searchable, and useful for various cognitive and practical purposes.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Organizational structures** (hierarchies, networks, matrices, trees, graphs, taxonomies)
- **Categorization systems** (classification, typologies, schemas, frameworks, inventories)
- **Information relationships** (associations, dependencies, causation, similarity, sequence)
- **Data formats** (lists, tables, databases, documents, files, records, entries)
- **Structural elements** (headers, sections, chapters, indices, cross-references, links)
- **Organization principles** (alphabetical, chronological, topical, hierarchical, geographical)
- **Information architecture** (navigation, search, filtering, sorting, grouping, tagging)
- **Knowledge organization** (ontologies, concept maps, mental models, semantic networks)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of effective information organization and can create and navigate various information structures. They recognize when information is well-organized versus chaotic and can use organizational principles to manage complex information.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Knowledge Representation" (which focuses on meaning rather than structure) and "Memory and Learning" (which emphasizes cognitive processes rather than information organization).

## 3. Media and Digital Systems

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of technological systems for creating, transmitting, and manipulating information and representations. It captures how humans conceptualize digital technologies, electronic media, and the various technological tools that extend human representational and communicative capabilities.

### Justification

Digital and media technologies have become fundamental to modern human communication and representation. Humans naturally adapt to and understand new technological systems for information processing. This subdomain is essential because it represents the technological extension of human representational capabilities that increasingly mediate human experience and social interaction.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Digital technologies** (computers, smartphones, internet, software, applications, platforms)
- **Media formats** (text, audio, video, images, multimedia, interactive media, virtual reality)
- **Communication technologies** (email, messaging, social media, video conferencing, forums)
- **Information processing** (data entry, editing, searching, filtering, manipulation, analysis)
- **Digital interfaces** (screens, keyboards, touchscreens, voice commands, gesture controls)
- **Network systems** (internet, wireless, cloud computing, distributed systems, connectivity)
- **Digital content** (websites, digital documents, databases, digital libraries, online resources)
- **Technology interaction** (user experience, digital literacy, human-computer interaction, automation)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of digital systems and can adapt to new technologies for representational and communicative purposes. They understand the affordances and limitations of different digital tools and can use technology strategically.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Formal Systems and Abstract Representations" (which focuses on abstract rather than technological systems) and "Signs and Communication and Language" (which emphasizes symbolic rather than technological aspects).

## 4. Formal Systems and Abstract Representations

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of systematic, rule-based representational systems that use abstract symbols and logical relationships to model reality or manipulate information. It encompasses mathematical systems, logical frameworks, and other formal representational tools that enable precise, systematic thinking and analysis.

### Justification

Formal systems represent a distinctive human cognitive achievement that enables precise reasoning and systematic knowledge. Humans can understand and work with abstract symbolic systems that follow explicit rules. This subdomain is essential because it represents the most systematic and powerful forms of human representation that enable scientific, mathematical, and logical thinking.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Mathematical systems** (arithmetic, algebra, geometry, calculus, statistics, mathematical logic)
- **Logical systems** (propositional logic, predicate logic, modal logic, reasoning systems)
- **Formal notations** (mathematical notation, logical symbols, scientific formulas, programming languages)
- **Abstract models** (mathematical models, theoretical frameworks, formal theories, axiomatic systems)
- **Systematic procedures** (algorithms, proofs, calculations, derivations, systematic methods)
- **Rule-based systems** (formal rules, syntactic systems, transformation rules, inference rules)
- **Precision tools** (measurement systems, quantification, exactness, formal definitions)
- **Abstract relationships** (functions, mappings, equivalences, transformations, isomorphisms)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans can learn to work with formal systems and understand the relationship between abstract representations and real-world phenomena. They develop appreciation for precision, systematicity, and the power of formal reasoning.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Reasoning and Problem-Solving" (which focuses on cognitive processes rather than formal systems) and "Information Structures" (which emphasizes organization rather than formal systematicity).

## 5. Knowledge Representation

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of how knowledge, concepts, and meanings are encoded, stored, and expressed in various representational formats. It captures how humans conceptualize the relationship between mental content and its external representation, including the various ways knowledge can be captured, organized, and communicated.

### Justification

Knowledge representation is fundamental to human learning, teaching, and cultural transmission. Humans naturally create external representations of their knowledge and understand how different representational formats affect understanding. This subdomain is essential because it represents the interface between internal mental content and external symbolic systems that enable knowledge preservation and sharing.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Representational formats** (concepts, definitions, descriptions, explanations, models, theories)
- **Knowledge structures** (schemas, frameworks, ontologies, concept maps, knowledge graphs)
- **Semantic relationships** (is-a relationships, part-whole, cause-effect, similarity, opposition)
- **Conceptual organization** (categories, hierarchies, networks, associations, semantic fields)
- **Knowledge encoding** (documentation, notation, representation languages, knowledge bases)
- **Meaning representation** (semantic content, propositional knowledge, conceptual knowledge)
- **Knowledge artifacts** (encyclopedias, textbooks, manuals, reference works, knowledge systems)
- **Representational adequacy** (completeness, accuracy, clarity, usability, expressiveness)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans understand how knowledge can be represented in different formats and can evaluate the adequacy of different representations for different purposes. They can translate between different representational formats and understand the relationship between representation and understanding.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Beliefs and Knowledge and Assumptions" (which focuses on cognitive content rather than its representation) and "Information Structures" (which emphasizes organization rather than meaning representation).

---

## Summary

The Representational domain captures the various systems and processes through which humans create, manipulate, and transmit symbolic representations of reality and meaning. These five subdomains progress from basic communicative representation (Signs and Communication and Language), through information organization (Information Structures), technological mediation (Media and Digital Systems), systematic formal representation (Formal Systems and Abstract Representations), to meaning encoding and knowledge preservation (Knowledge Representation). Together, they represent the full spectrum of human representational capabilities that enable complex thinking, communication, and cultural transmission.
