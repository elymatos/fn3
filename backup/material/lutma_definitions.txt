Entity: Frames that represent objects, actors, artifacts, concepts, or any discrete unit that can be conceptualized as an entity. Entities may be concrete (person, city) or abstract (law, algorithm, contract).                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      Event: Frames that capture dynamic situations: actions, operations, procedures, natural phenomena, or social events. They involve change over time, interaction between entities, or sequences of operations.
Process: Frames that describe a complex event which lasts some amount of time, consisting of a beginning stage, a stage where the process is ongoing, and a finish or end. In some cases the process may pause, and then possibly resume.
State: Frames that describe static situations, conditions, configurations, or properties that hold over time without inherent change. Examples include being hungry, ownership, or spatial positioning.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               Attribute: Frames representing attributes or characteristics of entities or states. These include measurable properties (size, weight), perceptual qualities (color, texture), and abstract attributes (importance, legality, validity).                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  Relation: Frames that explicitly represent the connection between entities, events, or states. Relations may be spatial (next to, inside), temporal (before, during), causal (cause-effect), hierarchical (part-of, type-of), or logical (equivalence, implication).                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     Image Schema: Primitive and recurrent cognitive representations that structure both perception and abstract reasoning. They serve as foundations for conceptual metaphors, spatial relations, dynamic processes, and event organization.


Entity: The definition is precise and captures the intended breadth well—spanning concrete and abstract entities. It correctly frames entities as discrete conceptual units.

Event: Clearly described in terms of dynamism, change, and interaction over time. This aligns well with FrameNet's use of event frames and could help distinguish from states and processes.

Process: This is effectively delineated as a subclass or a specific kind of event with temporal structure and possible interruption. The inclusion of stages (beginning, ongoing, end) is very useful for distinguishing processes from simpler events or from states.

State: Appropriately defined as static and temporally extended without inherent change, which will be crucial in distinguishing from both events and processes in FrameNet.

Attribute: Clearly delineates properties that modify or qualify entities or states. The inclusion of measurable, perceptual, and abstract dimensions is helpful.

Relation: Well-scoped. Capturing logical, spatial, temporal, causal, and hierarchical aspects ensures sufficient granularity to align with various relational frames in FrameNet.

Image Schema: This is perhaps the most theoretically dense category but is accurately presented as foundational cognitive structures. The emphasis on their preconceptual nature and role in metaphor and event understanding is aligned with cognitive semantics.
