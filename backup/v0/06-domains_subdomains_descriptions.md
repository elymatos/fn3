# Physical Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Matter and Substances

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human conceptualization of the material composition of reality, including the fundamental building blocks of the physical world and their perceived properties. It captures how humans categorize and understand different types of matter based on observable characteristics, composition, and behavioral properties.

### Justification

From a cognitive perspective, humans naturally categorize the physical world into discrete substance types based on sensory experience and functional properties. This subdomain is essential because it represents the foundational level of physical reality that humans interact with directly through touch, sight, smell, and taste. It forms the basis for tool-making, cooking, construction, and countless other human activities.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Metals** (iron, gold, copper) - characterized by hardness, conductivity, malleability
- **Liquids** (water, oil, blood) - flowing substances with varying viscosity and transparency
- **Gases** (air, steam, smoke) - invisible or visible vaporous substances
- **Organic materials** (wood, leather, bone) - substances derived from living things
- **Minerals and stones** (granite, quartz, clay) - hard, enduring earth-based materials
- **Composites** (concrete, plastic, alloys) - human-made combinations of substances
- **Powders and granules** (sand, flour, salt) - particulate forms of matter
- **Biological substances** (flesh, plant matter, bodily fluids) - matter from living systems

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop intuitive physics about substances through direct manipulation and observation. We form mental models about density, hardness, temperature, texture, and chemical reactivity that guide our interactions with the material world.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physical Transformation" (which focuses on processes of change) and "Biological Entities" (which emphasizes living systems rather than their material composition).

---

## 2. Motion and Location

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of movement, positioning, and spatial relationships in the physical world. It encompasses both the static concept of where things are located and the dynamic concept of how things move through space, as perceived and conceptualized by human cognition.

### Justification

Motion and spatial location are fundamental to human survival and navigation. Our cognitive systems are specifically evolved to track moving objects, predict trajectories, and understand spatial relationships. This subdomain is crucial because it represents how humans parse the continuous flow of movement into discrete, meaningful patterns and locations.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Types of motion** (walking, running, falling, rotating, vibrating)
- **Directional movement** (ascending, descending, approaching, retreating)
- **Positional relationships** (above, below, inside, adjacent, behind)
- **Trajectories** (straight line, curved path, circular motion, zigzag)
- **Speed variations** (accelerating, decelerating, constant velocity)
- **Relative motion** (following, chasing, orbiting, parallel movement)
- **Positional states** (stationary, balanced, tilted, suspended)
- **Spatial boundaries** (edges, surfaces, perimeters, centers)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans possess sophisticated mental mechanisms for tracking motion and understanding spatial relationships, including motion perception, spatial memory, and predictive capabilities about future positions of moving objects.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Spatial Configuration" (which focuses on abstract spatial structures) and "Motion and Trajectory" in Space-Time domain (which emphasizes temporal aspects of movement).

---

## 3. Physical Transformation

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses processes of physical change that alter the form, state, or properties of matter without necessarily changing its fundamental identity. It captures how humans understand and categorize the various ways physical entities can be modified, combined, or altered through natural or intentional processes.

### Justification

Understanding physical transformation is essential for human technology, cooking, craftsmanship, and interaction with the environment. Humans naturally categorize transformation processes based on observable changes and reversibility. This subdomain is necessary because it represents a distinct category of physical phenomena—processes rather than states or entities.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **State changes** (melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, sublimation)
- **Mechanical transformations** (breaking, cutting, grinding, shaping, molding)
- **Thermal processes** (heating, cooling, burning, charring)
- **Chemical-physical changes** (rusting, fermentation, decay, crystallization)
- **Mixing and separation** (dissolving, filtering, distillation, precipitation)
- **Deformation** (stretching, compressing, bending, twisting)
- **Assembly and disassembly** (construction, demolition, joining, splitting)
- **Weathering processes** (erosion, oxidation, wear, aging)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop causal models of transformation that allow prediction and control of physical processes. These mental models are crucial for tool use, manufacturing, and environmental adaptation.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Matter and Substances" (which focuses on static properties) and "Natural Phenomena" (which emphasizes large-scale environmental processes rather than controllable transformations).

---

## 4. Natural Phenomena

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses large-scale physical events and processes that occur in the natural environment, typically beyond direct human control. It captures how humans perceive, categorize, and understand weather patterns, geological events, astronomical phenomena, and other manifestations of natural forces.

### Justification

Natural phenomena represent a distinct category in human cognition because they are experienced as powerful, often unpredictable forces that influence human life but cannot be directly controlled. Humans have evolved sophisticated pattern recognition for these phenomena as they are crucial for survival, navigation, and agricultural planning.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Weather phenomena** (rain, snow, wind, storms, lightning, rainbows)
- **Geological events** (earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, landslides, tsunamis)
- **Astronomical phenomena** (sunrise, sunset, eclipses, meteor showers, tides)
- **Seasonal patterns** (spring blooming, autumn leaf change, winter dormancy)
- **Atmospheric effects** (fog, mist, aurora, mirages, optical illusions)
- **Water phenomena** (waterfalls, geysers, hot springs, floods, droughts)
- **Fire phenomena** (wildfires, spontaneous combustion, electrical discharges)
- **Magnetic and electrical** (magnetic fields, static electricity, natural radiation)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans have developed sophisticated pattern recognition for natural phenomena, often attributing agency or intentionality to these processes. They form the basis for many cultural beliefs, agricultural practices, and navigation techniques.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physical Transformation" (which involves controllable processes) and "Ecological Systems" (which focuses on living systems and their interactions).

---

## 5. Manipulation and Interaction with Objects

### Definition

This subdomain captures the various ways humans physically engage with, modify, and control objects in their environment. It encompasses both the actions humans perform on objects and the cognitive understanding of how objects can be manipulated, used as tools, or arranged to achieve specific goals.

### Justification

Object manipulation is fundamental to human survival and culture. This subdomain is essential because it represents the interface between human agency and the physical world. It captures not just the actions themselves, but the cognitive schemas humans develop about affordances, tool use, and object relationships.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Basic manipulations** (grasping, lifting, pushing, pulling, carrying, throwing)
- **Tool use** (cutting with knives, hammering, digging, measuring, writing)
- **Fine motor control** (threading, drawing, carving, assembling small parts)
- **Object arrangement** (stacking, organizing, aligning, sorting, grouping)
- **Force application** (pressing, squeezing, twisting, leverage, mechanical advantage)
- **Construction activities** (building, joining, fastening, weaving, molding)
- **Destructive actions** (breaking, tearing, crushing, dismantling)
- **Precision operations** (aiming, balancing, calibrating, adjusting)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated mental models of object affordances—understanding what actions objects support and how they can be used as tools. This includes understanding of physics principles like leverage, balance, and mechanical advantage.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physical Transformation" (which focuses on changes to materials) and "Intention and Goal-Oriented Action" (which emphasizes cognitive planning rather than physical execution).

---

## Summary

The Physical domain establishes the foundational layer of human interaction with material reality. These five subdomains capture different aspects of physicality: the composition of things (Matter and Substances), their spatial dynamics (Motion and Location), their mutability (Physical Transformation), the larger natural forces at work (Natural Phenomena), and human agency in relation to physical objects (Manipulation and Interaction with Objects).

# Biological Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Biological Entities and Life Processes

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human conceptualization of living beings and the fundamental processes that characterize life. It captures how humans categorize and understand organisms, their life cycles, and the essential activities that distinguish living from non-living entities, based on observable behaviors and intuitive biological knowledge.

### Justification

Humans possess innate cognitive mechanisms for detecting and categorizing living entities, often called "folk biology." This subdomain is essential because it represents one of the most fundamental cognitive distinctions humans make—the animate versus inanimate. Understanding life processes is crucial for survival, as it enables recognition of food sources, predators, mates, and disease vectors.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Organisms by type** (animals, plants, fungi, microorganisms, parasites)
- **Life cycle stages** (birth, growth, reproduction, aging, death)
- **Vital processes** (breathing, feeding, metabolism, circulation, excretion)
- **Reproductive processes** (mating, pregnancy, laying eggs, pollination, spawning)
- **Growth and development** (germination, maturation, molting, flowering)
- **Species interactions** (predation, symbiosis, competition, cooperation)
- **Biological inheritance** (genetics, heredity, family resemblance, breeding)
- **Adaptation mechanisms** (evolution, natural selection, survival strategies)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans naturally categorize living things into folk taxonomies based on morphological similarity, behavior patterns, and ecological relationships. They develop intuitive understanding of biological causation, including growth, reproduction, and species-typical behaviors.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Body and Anatomy" (which focuses on structural components) and "Ecological Systems" (which emphasizes environmental relationships rather than individual organisms and processes).

---

## 2. Health and Illness

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the spectrum from wellness to disease, including the recognition, categorization, and management of health states. It encompasses both physical and mental health conditions as they are experienced, perceived, and culturally understood by humans, rather than purely medical definitions.

### Justification

Health and illness represent a fundamental dimension of human experience that shapes behavior, social relationships, and life planning. Humans have evolved sophisticated mechanisms for detecting signs of illness in themselves and others. This subdomain is crucial because it represents embodied knowledge about bodily states that guides self-care, social behavior, and help-seeking.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Health states** (wellness, vitality, fitness, robustness, energy levels)
- **Illness types** (infections, injuries, chronic conditions, genetic disorders)
- **Symptoms and signs** (pain, fever, fatigue, inflammation, weakness)
- **Mental health conditions** (depression, anxiety, stress, cognitive decline)
- **Recovery processes** (healing, convalescence, rehabilitation, remission)
- **Preventive measures** (hygiene, exercise, nutrition, immunization, rest)
- **Health threats** (contagion, toxins, environmental hazards, aging)
- **Disability and impairment** (sensory loss, mobility limitations, cognitive deficits)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated body awareness and symptom recognition. They form mental models about disease causation, transmission, and treatment that guide health behaviors and social interactions during illness.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physiological Functions" (which focuses on normal biological processes) and "Emotions and Affective States" (though psychological wellbeing overlaps, this focuses specifically on health-illness dimensions).

---

## 3. Body and Anatomy

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human conceptualization of bodily structure, including the recognition, naming, and understanding of body parts, their spatial relationships, and their functional organization. It captures both external anatomy visible to everyday observation and internal structures understood through embodied experience.

### Justification

Body awareness is fundamental to human consciousness and identity. Humans develop detailed mental maps of their own bodies and recognize anatomical structures in others. This subdomain is essential because it represents the primary interface between consciousness and physical existence, shaping concepts of self, identity, and capability.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **External body parts** (head, limbs, torso, facial features, skin, hair)
- **Internal organs** (heart, lungs, brain, stomach, liver, kidneys)
- **Body systems** (skeletal, muscular, nervous, circulatory, digestive)
- **Sensory organs** (eyes, ears, nose, mouth, skin receptors)
- **Body cavities and spaces** (chest, abdomen, joints, body openings)
- **Body proportions** (symmetry, size relationships, anatomical landmarks)
- **Gender-specific anatomy** (reproductive organs, secondary sex characteristics)
- **Developmental anatomy** (infant, child, adult, elderly body configurations)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans possess detailed proprioceptive and interoceptive awareness of their bodies. They develop mental body schemas that guide movement, self-recognition, and understanding of physical capabilities and limitations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physiological Functions" (which focuses on processes rather than structures) and "Subjective Experience of the Body" (which emphasizes felt experience rather than anatomical knowledge).

---

## 4. Physiological Functions

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the ongoing biological processes that maintain life and enable bodily functions. It encompasses the recognition and conceptualization of automatic and voluntary bodily processes, from basic vital functions to complex coordinated activities.

### Justification

Understanding physiological functions is crucial for human survival and wellbeing. Humans develop intuitive knowledge about bodily processes through direct experience and observation. This subdomain is necessary because it represents functional rather than structural aspects of biology—the "how" rather than the "what" of bodily existence.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Vital functions** (heartbeat, breathing, circulation, digestion, excretion)
- **Sensory processes** (seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching)
- **Motor functions** (walking, grasping, balance, coordination, reflexes)
- **Regulatory processes** (temperature control, sleep-wake cycles, hunger-satiety)
- **Cognitive functions** (attention, memory, perception, consciousness)
- **Reproductive functions** (sexual arousal, menstruation, lactation, fertility)
- **Immune responses** (inflammation, wound healing, disease resistance)
- **Developmental processes** (growth spurts, puberty, aging changes)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans monitor their own physiological states through interoceptive awareness and learn to recognize normal versus abnormal functioning. They develop predictive models about bodily needs and responses.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Body and Anatomy" (which focuses on structure) and "Health and Illness" (which emphasizes pathological rather than normal functioning).

---

## 5. Ecological Systems

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the interconnected relationships between living organisms and their environments, including concepts of habitats, ecosystems, and environmental interdependence. It captures how humans perceive and categorize the complex web of relationships that sustain life on multiple scales.

### Justification

Humans have evolved as part of ecological systems and possess intuitive understanding of environmental relationships crucial for survival. This subdomain is essential because it represents systems-level thinking about biology—understanding organisms not as isolated entities but as part of larger living systems. This knowledge guides resource management, agricultural practices, and environmental stewardship.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Habitat types** (forests, grasslands, wetlands, deserts, marine environments)
- **Ecological relationships** (food chains, predator-prey, pollination, decomposition)
- **Environmental cycles** (seasonal changes, water cycle, nutrient cycling)
- **Biodiversity patterns** (species richness, endemic species, invasive species)
- **Resource flows** (energy transfer, material cycling, migration patterns)
- **Environmental pressures** (climate, competition, limited resources, natural disasters)
- **Human-environment interaction** (agriculture, conservation, pollution, habitat modification)
- **Ecosystem services** (pollination, water purification, climate regulation, soil formation)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop mental models of environmental relationships and resource availability. They recognize patterns of seasonal change, species associations, and environmental indicators that guide foraging, farming, and habitat selection.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Natural Phenomena" (which focuses on physical rather than biological systems) and "Biological Entities and Life Processes" (which emphasizes individual organisms rather than system-level relationships).

---

## Summary

The Biological domain captures human understanding of life in its various manifestations. These five subdomains progress from individual organisms and their processes (Biological Entities and Life Processes), through the experience of biological wellness and dysfunction (Health and Illness), to the structural (Body and Anatomy) and functional (Physiological Functions) aspects of living bodies, and finally to the larger ecological context in which life exists (Ecological Systems). Together, they represent the cognitive foundation for human interaction with the living world.

# Social Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Social Roles and Identities

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the various positions, functions, and identities that individuals occupy within social structures. It captures how humans conceptualize social personas, status positions, and the behavioral expectations associated with different social categories and relationships.

### Justification

Social role recognition is fundamental to human social cognition. Humans automatically categorize others based on perceived social roles and adjust their behavior accordingly. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans navigate complex social hierarchies and relationships through role-based expectations and identity markers. Social roles provide predictable frameworks for interaction and cooperation.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Family roles** (parent, child, sibling, grandparent, spouse, in-law)
- **Professional roles** (teacher, doctor, manager, employee, entrepreneur, artist)
- **Community roles** (leader, elder, newcomer, neighbor, volunteer, citizen)
- **Age-based identities** (infant, child, teenager, adult, senior)
- **Gender identities** (masculine, feminine, non-binary expressions and roles)
- **Social status markers** (wealthy, poor, educated, prestigious, marginalized)
- **Cultural identities** (ethnic, religious, regional, national affiliations)
- **Achieved vs. ascribed roles** (earned positions vs. inherited status)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans possess sophisticated social categorization abilities that enable rapid role recognition and appropriate behavioral responses. They develop mental schemas about role expectations, status hierarchies, and identity markers that guide social interaction.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Social Interaction and Relationships" (which focuses on processes rather than positions) and "Heritage and Identity" (which emphasizes cultural rather than social structural aspects).

---

## 2. Institutions and Organizations

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of formal and informal social structures that organize collective activities, establish rules, and coordinate group behavior. It encompasses how humans conceptualize, participate in, and navigate various organizational forms that structure social life.

### Justification

Institutional thinking is crucial for human cooperation at scale. Humans naturally recognize and create organizational structures to solve collective problems. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans understand and operate within complex social systems that extend beyond immediate personal relationships, enabling large-scale coordination and cultural transmission.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Educational institutions** (schools, universities, libraries, training centers)
- **Religious organizations** (churches, temples, monasteries, religious orders)
- **Government bodies** (legislatures, courts, bureaucracies, military, police)
- **Economic institutions** (markets, banks, corporations, trade unions, cooperatives)
- **Healthcare systems** (hospitals, clinics, insurance, medical associations)
- **Cultural institutions** (museums, theaters, media organizations, festivals)
- **Social service organizations** (charities, NGOs, community centers, support groups)
- **Informal institutions** (clubs, societies, networks, traditions, customs)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop mental models of institutional structures, hierarchies, and procedures. They understand institutional roles, power relationships, and the formal and informal rules that govern organizational behavior.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Governance and Law" (which focuses on regulatory rather than organizational aspects) and "Economic and Commercial Practices" (which emphasizes market activities rather than institutional structures).

---

## 3. Economic and Commercial Practices

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of exchange, trade, and economic relationships that involve the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services. It captures how humans conceptualize value, transaction, and the various forms of economic interaction in their social environment.

### Justification

Economic thinking is fundamental to human survival and cooperation. Humans naturally engage in reciprocal exchange and understand concepts of value, fairness, and economic obligation. This subdomain is essential because it represents a distinct domain of social interaction organized around resource allocation, trade, and economic relationships that shape much of social life.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Exchange mechanisms** (buying, selling, trading, bartering, gifting, sharing)
- **Financial concepts** (money, credit, debt, investment, savings, insurance)
- **Market activities** (shopping, negotiating, advertising, competing, pricing)
- **Work and labor** (employment, wages, contracts, business ownership, entrepreneurship)
- **Economic relationships** (customer-vendor, employer-employee, landlord-tenant)
- **Value concepts** (cost, profit, loss, worth, affordability, luxury vs. necessity)
- **Economic institutions** (banking, taxation, property rights, market regulation)
- **Resource management** (budgeting, planning, allocation, conservation, waste)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop intuitive understanding of economic value, fairness in exchange, and reciprocity obligations. They form mental models about market dynamics, resource scarcity, and economic opportunity that guide financial decision-making.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Institutions and Organizations" (which focuses on structural rather than transactional aspects) and "Governance and Law" (which emphasizes regulation rather than market activities).

---

## 4. Governance and Law

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of systems of authority, rule-making, and social control that regulate collective behavior. It encompasses how humans conceptualize legitimate authority, legal obligations, and the various mechanisms societies use to establish and enforce social order.

### Justification

Understanding authority and rules is fundamental to human social cooperation. Humans naturally recognize legitimate authority and understand concepts of rules, violations, and consequences. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans navigate power structures and regulatory systems that coordinate large-scale social behavior and resolve conflicts.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Authority structures** (leadership, hierarchy, sovereignty, jurisdiction, mandate)
- **Legal concepts** (laws, rights, obligations, contracts, property, citizenship)
- **Justice systems** (courts, trials, judges, juries, punishment, rehabilitation)
- **Enforcement mechanisms** (police, military, penalties, sanctions, compliance)
- **Political processes** (voting, representation, legislation, policy-making, advocacy)
- **Regulatory frameworks** (permits, licenses, standards, inspections, violations)
- **Dispute resolution** (mediation, arbitration, litigation, negotiation, appeals)
- **Constitutional concepts** (fundamental rights, separation of powers, checks and balances)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of legitimate authority, rule-following obligations, and fairness in justice systems. They recognize power relationships and understand the social contract that governs collective life.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Institutions and Organizations" (which focuses on organizational rather than regulatory aspects) and "Moral" domain concepts (which emphasize ethical rather than legal frameworks).

---

## 5. Social Interaction and Relationships

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses the dynamic processes through which humans connect, communicate, and form relationships with one another. It captures the various forms of social engagement, from brief encounters to deep personal bonds, and the behavioral patterns that characterize human social connection.

### Justification

Social interaction is the fundamental building block of human society. Humans are inherently social beings who form complex networks of relationships. This subdomain is essential because it represents the processual, dynamic aspects of social life—how relationships are formed, maintained, and dissolved, and how social bonds influence behavior and wellbeing.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Relationship types** (friendship, romance, marriage, kinship, mentorship, rivalry)
- **Social behaviors** (greeting, conversation, cooperation, competition, helping, sharing)
- **Communication patterns** (talking, listening, arguing, persuading, comforting, joking)
- **Emotional connections** (love, trust, loyalty, jealousy, attachment, intimacy)
- **Social networks** (family circles, friend groups, professional networks, communities)
- **Relationship dynamics** (bonding, conflict, reconciliation, separation, reunion)
- **Social rituals** (introductions, celebrations, ceremonies, farewells, traditions)
- **Group membership** (inclusion, exclusion, belonging, acceptance, rejection)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans possess sophisticated social cognition for reading social cues, understanding relationship dynamics, and navigating complex interpersonal situations. They develop mental models of relationship quality and social obligations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Social Roles and Identities" (which focuses on positions rather than processes) and "Group Dynamics and Collective Behavior" (which emphasizes group-level rather than interpersonal phenomena).

---

## 6. Conflict and Cooperation

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the spectrum from collaborative to competitive social dynamics, including the recognition, management, and resolution of social tensions. It encompasses both constructive cooperation and destructive conflict as fundamental patterns of human social interaction.

### Justification

Conflict and cooperation represent fundamental social dynamics that shape all human societies. Humans naturally recognize competitive versus collaborative situations and adjust their strategies accordingly. This subdomain is essential because it represents a distinct dimension of social interaction focused on the alignment or opposition of interests and goals.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Cooperative behaviors** (teamwork, collaboration, mutual aid, alliance-building, sharing resources)
- **Competitive dynamics** (rivalry, competition, contest, struggle for resources or status)
- **Conflict types** (arguments, disputes, feuds, wars, legal battles, ideological conflicts)
- **Resolution mechanisms** (negotiation, compromise, mediation, peace-making, reconciliation)
- **Alliance patterns** (partnership, coalition, treaty, mutual defense, joint ventures)
- **Aggressive behaviors** (fighting, bullying, intimidation, retaliation, revenge)
- **Peaceful coexistence** (tolerance, diplomacy, neutrality, peaceful settlement)
- **Power struggles** (dominance, submission, resistance, rebellion, revolution)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop strategic thinking about when to cooperate versus compete, how to form alliances, and how to manage conflicts. They understand concepts of fairness, reciprocity, and justice in social interactions.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Social Interaction and Relationships" (which focuses on general relational processes) and "Governance and Law" (which emphasizes formal rather than interpersonal conflict resolution).

---

## 7. Group Dynamics and Collective Behavior

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of how groups form, function, and influence individual behavior. It captures the emergent properties of collective action, group psychology, and the various ways that being part of a group changes individual cognition and behavior.

### Justification

Group psychology is fundamental to human social life. Humans naturally form groups and are influenced by group membership in profound ways. This subdomain is essential because it represents emergent social phenomena that cannot be reduced to individual psychology or simple interpersonal relationships—the distinct dynamics that arise when humans act collectively.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Group formation** (joining, initiation, membership criteria, group identity, solidarity)
- **Collective action** (protests, movements, mob behavior, crowd dynamics, mass events)
- **Group decision-making** (consensus, voting, deliberation, leadership, influence)
- **Social pressure** (conformity, peer pressure, social norms, groupthink, dissent)
- **Group roles** (leaders, followers, facilitators, disruptors, outsiders, scapegoats)
- **Collective identity** (team spirit, group loyalty, in-group/out-group distinctions)
- **Mass phenomena** (trends, fashions, panics, rumors, collective memory)
- **Organizational behavior** (workplace culture, team dynamics, institutional culture)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans understand how group membership affects behavior and develop strategies for navigating group dynamics. They recognize when individual versus group psychology is dominant and adjust their behavior accordingly.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Social Interaction and Relationships" (which focuses on interpersonal rather than group-level dynamics) and "Institutions and Organizations" (which emphasizes formal structures rather than group psychology).

---

## Summary

The Social domain captures the fundamental ways humans organize collective life. These seven subdomains progress from individual social positioning (Social Roles and Identities), through formal organizational structures (Institutions and Organizations), to specific domains of social activity (Economic and Commercial Practices, Governance and Law), and finally to the dynamic processes of social interaction (Social Interaction and Relationships, Conflict and Cooperation, Group Dynamics and Collective Behavior). Together, they represent the cognitive foundation for understanding human society and social behavior.

# Cultural Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Beliefs and Worldviews

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of fundamental perspectives on reality, existence, and meaning that shape how individuals and groups interpret their world. It captures the deep cognitive frameworks, belief systems, and philosophical orientations that guide human understanding of life's fundamental questions and provide interpretive lenses for experience.

### Justification

Worldviews and belief systems are fundamental to human cognition and social organization. Humans naturally form comprehensive frameworks for understanding reality that go beyond immediate empirical experience. This subdomain is essential because it represents the foundational cognitive structures that influence all other aspects of cultural life—how humans make sense of existence, morality, causation, and their place in the universe.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Religious beliefs** (monotheism, polytheism, animism, atheism, agnosticism, spirituality)
- **Cosmological views** (creation stories, afterlife concepts, divine intervention, fate vs. free will)
- **Philosophical orientations** (materialism, idealism, existentialism, determinism, humanism)
- **Explanatory frameworks** (scientific rationalism, mysticism, traditionalism, supernaturalism)
- **Value systems** (individualism vs. collectivism, progress vs. tradition, sacred vs. secular)
- **Meaning-making concepts** (purpose of life, suffering, justice, truth, beauty, goodness)
- **Epistemological beliefs** (how knowledge is acquired, sources of authority, truth criteria)
- **Metaphysical concepts** (nature of reality, consciousness, time, causation, unity vs. diversity)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop coherent worldview frameworks that provide cognitive scaffolding for interpreting experiences and making decisions. These belief systems offer psychological security, social identity, and behavioral guidance in uncertain situations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Moral" domain concepts (which focus on ethical evaluation rather than fundamental reality interpretation) and "Knowledge Representation" (which emphasizes information structure rather than belief content).

---

## 2. Rituals and Traditions

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of ceremonial practices, customary behaviors, and repeated cultural activities that mark significant events, maintain social bonds, and transmit cultural knowledge. It encompasses both formal religious ceremonies and informal cultural practices that structure social life through symbolic action.

### Justification

Ritual behavior is universal across human cultures and serves crucial social and psychological functions. Humans naturally create and participate in patterned, symbolic activities that build group cohesion and mark important transitions. This subdomain is essential because it represents how abstract cultural meanings are embodied in concrete actions and how cultural knowledge is transmitted through practice rather than explicit instruction.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Life cycle rituals** (birth ceremonies, coming of age, weddings, funerals, baptisms)
- **Religious ceremonies** (worship services, prayer rituals, pilgrimage, communion, meditation)
- **Seasonal celebrations** (harvest festivals, new year celebrations, solstice observances)
- **Community traditions** (parades, festivals, commemorations, reunions, local customs)
- **Daily rituals** (meal practices, greeting customs, bedtime routines, work traditions)
- **Ceremonial objects** (sacred items, regalia, ceremonial tools, symbolic artifacts)
- **Rites of passage** (initiation ceremonies, graduation, ordination, retirement celebrations)
- **Commemorative practices** (memorial services, anniversary observances, historical reenactments)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans understand rituals as meaningful, rule-governed activities that create social connection and mark temporal boundaries. They recognize the symbolic significance of ceremonial actions and their role in maintaining cultural continuity.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Beliefs and Worldviews" (which focuses on cognitive content rather than behavioral practice) and "Social Interaction and Relationships" (which emphasizes interpersonal rather than cultural-symbolic dimensions).

---

## 3. Arts and Creative Practices

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of creative expression, aesthetic appreciation, and the various forms of artistic production that communicate meaning, emotion, and cultural values through symbolic representation. It captures how humans conceptualize beauty, creativity, and the role of artistic expression in cultural life.

### Justification

Together, they represent the core components of human psychological life as experienced from the first-person perspective.

# Cognitive Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 5. Knowledge Representation

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of how knowledge, concepts, and meanings are encoded, stored, and expressed in various representational formats. It captures how humans conceptualize the relationship between mental content and its external representation, including the various ways knowledge can be captured, organized, and communicated.

### Justification

Knowledge representation is fundamental to human learning, teaching, and cultural transmission. Humans naturally create external representations of their knowledge and understand how different representational formats affect understanding. This subdomain is essential because it represents the interface between internal mental content and external symbolic systems that enable knowledge preservation and sharing.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Representational formats** (concepts, definitions, descriptions, explanations, models, theories)
- **Knowledge structures** (schemas, frameworks, ontologies, concept maps, knowledge graphs)
- **Semantic relationships** (is-a relationships, part-whole, cause-effect, similarity, opposition)
- **Conceptual organization** (categories, hierarchies, networks, associations, semantic fields)
- **Knowledge encoding** (documentation, notation, representation languages, knowledge bases)
- **Meaning representation** (semantic content, propositional knowledge, conceptual knowledge)
- **Knowledge artifacts** (encyclopedias, textbooks, manuals, reference works, knowledge systems)
- **Representational adequacy** (completeness, accuracy, clarity, usability, expressiveness)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans understand how knowledge can be represented in different formats and can evaluate the adequacy of different representations for different purposes. They can translate between different representational formats and understand the relationship between representation and understanding.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Beliefs and Knowledge and Assumptions" (which focuses on cognitive content rather than its representation) and "Information Structures" (which emphasizes organization rather than meaning representation).

---

## Summary

The Representational domain captures the various systems and processes through which humans create, manipulate, and transmit symbolic representations of reality and meaning. These five subdomains progress from basic communicative representation (Signs and Communication and Language), through information organization (Information Structures), technological mediation (Media and Digital Systems), systematic formal representation (Formal Systems and Abstract Representations), to meaning encoding and knowledge preservation (Knowledge Representation). Together, they represent the full spectrum of human representational capabilities that enable complex thinking, communication, and cultural transmission.

# Space-Time Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Spatial Configuration

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the arrangement, organization, and geometric relationships of objects and spaces. It captures how humans conceptualize spatial structures, layouts, and the various ways that entities can be positioned, oriented, and arranged in relation to one another within spatial frameworks.

### Justification

Spatial configuration is fundamental to human navigation, planning, and environmental understanding. Humans naturally perceive and create spatial arrangements and understand geometric relationships. This subdomain is essential because it represents the structural aspects of space that enable humans to organize their environment, navigate effectively, and understand complex spatial relationships independent of movement or change.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Geometric relationships** (parallel, perpendicular, intersecting, adjacent, overlapping, concentric)
- **Spatial arrangements** (patterns, layouts, configurations, formations, alignments, distributions)
- **Dimensional concepts** (one-dimensional, two-dimensional, three-dimensional, height, width, depth)
- **Architectural spaces** (rooms, buildings, courtyards, corridors, levels, compartments)
- **Landscape features** (terrain, topography, elevation, slopes, valleys, ridges, boundaries)
- **Positional relationships** (inside-outside, above-below, front-back, left-right, center-periphery)
- **Spatial scales** (microscopic, human-scale, architectural, geographical, cosmic)
- **Organizational principles** (symmetry, balance, proportion, hierarchy, zoning, clustering)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated spatial cognition that enables understanding of complex geometric relationships and spatial problem-solving. They can mentally manipulate spatial configurations and understand how spatial arrangements affect function and aesthetics.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Motion and Location" in the Physical domain (which focuses on dynamic positioning rather than static configuration) and "Motion and Trajectory" (which emphasizes movement rather than spatial structure).

---

## 2. Temporal Structure

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of how time is organized, structured, and experienced, including the various ways humans conceptualize temporal relationships, sequences, rhythms, and the organization of events within temporal frameworks. It encompasses both subjective time experience and objective temporal organization.

### Justification

Temporal structure is fundamental to human experience, planning, and cultural organization. Humans naturally organize time into meaningful units and understand temporal relationships. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans impose structure on the flow of time to create predictability, coordination, and meaning in temporal experience.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Temporal units** (moments, seconds, minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, years, decades)
- **Temporal cycles** (daily rhythms, weekly patterns, seasonal cycles, annual cycles, life cycles)
- **Temporal organization** (schedules, calendars, timetables, agendas, chronologies, timelines)
- **Temporal relationships** (before, after, during, simultaneous, sequential, overlapping)
- **Temporal patterns** (rhythms, periodicities, frequencies, intervals, repetitions, variations)
- **Time periods** (past, present, future, eras, epochs, ages, periods, phases)
- **Temporal boundaries** (beginnings, endings, transitions, deadlines, milestones, turning points)
- **Subjective time** (duration experience, time perception, temporal flow, temporal perspective)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated temporal cognition that enables planning, coordination, and understanding of temporal relationships. They can mentally organize events in time and understand how temporal structure affects experience and action.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Change over Time" (which focuses on transformation rather than temporal organization) and "Memory and Learning" (which emphasizes temporal aspects of cognition rather than time structure itself).

---

## 3. Motion and Trajectory

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of movement through space and time, including the paths, patterns, and dynamics of motion as experienced and conceptualized by humans. It captures how humans perceive, predict, and understand the temporal-spatial aspects of movement and the trajectories that moving entities follow.

### Justification

Motion perception and trajectory understanding are fundamental to human survival, navigation, and interaction with the physical world. Humans naturally track movement and predict trajectories. This subdomain is essential because it represents the dynamic integration of space and time that characterizes movement and enables humans to interact effectively with a world of moving objects and navigate through changing spatial environments.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Movement patterns** (straight-line motion, curved paths, circular motion, oscillation, spiral motion)
- **Trajectory characteristics** (direction, speed, acceleration, velocity, momentum, inertia)
- **Movement types** (translation, rotation, vibration, undulation, bouncing, flowing)
- **Path properties** (smooth, jagged, predictable, random, efficient, indirect, optimal)
- **Directional concepts** (toward, away from, north, south, up, down, forward, backward)
- **Dynamic relationships** (following, leading, converging, diverging, intercepting, avoiding)
- **Spatial-temporal integration** (distance over time, position changes, route planning, navigation)
- **Movement prediction** (trajectory extrapolation, collision prediction, arrival time estimation)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans possess sophisticated motion perception and trajectory prediction capabilities that enable navigation, sports performance, and interaction with moving objects. They understand the physics of motion and can plan and execute complex movements.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Motion and Location" in the Physical domain (which focuses on physical properties rather than trajectory patterns) and "Spatial Configuration" (which emphasizes static rather than dynamic spatial relationships).

---

## 4. Change over Time

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of transformation, development, and evolution as processes that unfold through time. It encompasses how humans conceptualize various forms of change, from gradual development to sudden transformation, and the temporal patterns that characterize different types of change processes.

### Justification

Understanding change over time is fundamental to human adaptation, learning, and planning. Humans naturally recognize patterns of change and can predict future states based on temporal trends. This subdomain is essential because it represents how humans understand the temporal dimension of transformation and development, enabling them to recognize patterns, predict outcomes, and plan interventions in ongoing processes.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Change patterns** (gradual change, sudden change, cyclical change, exponential change, linear change)
- **Developmental processes** (growth, maturation, aging, evolution, progress, regression)
- **Transformation types** (improvement, deterioration, adaptation, metamorphosis, revolution, reform)
- **Temporal dynamics** (acceleration, deceleration, persistence, fluctuation, stability, volatility)
- **Change trajectories** (upward trends, downward trends, plateaus, cycles, oscillations)
- **Causal sequences** (cause and effect over time, chain reactions, cascading effects, feedback loops)
- **Life processes** (birth, development, reproduction, death, regeneration, renewal)
- **Historical patterns** (trends, eras, epochs, transitions, turning points, continuities)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of change patterns and can recognize, predict, and influence change processes. They understand the relationship between time and transformation and can use temporal thinking to plan interventions and adaptations.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Physical Transformation" in the Physical domain (which focuses on material rather than temporal aspects of change) and "Temporal Structure" (which emphasizes time organization rather than change processes).

---

## Summary

The Space-Time domain captures the fundamental dimensions within which all human experience and action occur. These four subdomains represent how humans understand and navigate the spatial and temporal frameworks of reality: static spatial relationships (Spatial Configuration), temporal organization and structure (Temporal Structure), dynamic movement through space-time (Motion and Trajectory), and transformation processes over time (Change over Time). Together, they provide the foundational frameworks that enable humans to orient themselves in reality, coordinate action, and understand the dynamic nature of existence within spatial and temporal dimensions.

# Moral Domain - Subdomain Descriptions

## 1. Conventions and Ethical Norms and Principles

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of the rules, standards, and principles that guide moral behavior and define what is considered right, wrong, good, or bad within communities and cultures. It captures how humans conceptualize ethical frameworks, moral codes, and the normative systems that prescribe appropriate conduct and character.

### Justification

Moral norms and principles are fundamental to human social cooperation and individual character development. Humans naturally develop and follow ethical guidelines that enable coordinated social life. This subdomain is essential because it represents the prescriptive dimension of morality—the standards and principles that guide moral reasoning and provide frameworks for determining appropriate action and character.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Ethical principles** (honesty, fairness, compassion, integrity, respect, responsibility, loyalty)
- **Moral rules** (prohibitions against harm, requirements of care, duties of reciprocity)
- **Virtue concepts** (courage, temperance, wisdom, justice, kindness, humility, patience)
- **Vice concepts** (greed, cruelty, dishonesty, cowardice, pride, selfishness, injustice)
- **Normative frameworks** (deontological duties, consequentialist calculations, virtue ethics)
- **Social conventions** (politeness, etiquette, propriety, decorum, appropriate behavior)
- **Professional ethics** (medical ethics, business ethics, research ethics, legal ethics)
- **Universal vs. relative** (cross-cultural principles vs. culturally specific norms)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of moral principles and can apply ethical reasoning to novel situations. They recognize the difference between moral principles and social conventions and can engage in moral reasoning about competing values and principles.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Governance and Law" (which focuses on legal rather than ethical frameworks) and "Moral Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes assessment rather than normative principles).

---

## 2. Moral Judgments and Evaluations

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the processes through which moral assessments are made, including the evaluation of actions, intentions, character, and outcomes according to moral criteria. It encompasses how humans conceptualize moral reasoning, ethical decision-making, and the formation of moral judgments about rightness and wrongness.

### Justification

Moral judgment is fundamental to human ethical life and social coordination. Humans naturally evaluate actions and character according to moral criteria and make distinctions between right and wrong. This subdomain is essential because it represents the evaluative processes through which moral principles are applied to concrete situations and through which humans navigate ethical dilemmas and moral complexity.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Moral evaluations** (right vs. wrong, good vs. evil, virtuous vs. vicious, praiseworthy vs. blameworthy)
- **Judgment processes** (moral reasoning, ethical deliberation, conscience, moral intuition)
- **Moral considerations** (intentions, consequences, character, context, circumstances)
- **Ethical dilemmas** (competing values, tragic choices, moral conflicts, ethical trade-offs)
- **Moral responsibility** (accountability, culpability, praise, blame, moral agency)
- **Judgment criteria** (harm, fairness, rights, dignity, consequences, duties, virtues)
- **Moral complexity** (moral ambiguity, ethical uncertainty, competing claims, contextual factors)
- **Moral development** (moral maturity, ethical growth, moral learning, conscience formation)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop sophisticated moral reasoning abilities and can navigate complex ethical situations through careful moral evaluation. They understand the factors that affect moral judgment and can engage in reflective moral thinking.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Conventions and Ethical Norms and Principles" (which focuses on standards rather than evaluation processes) and "Cognitive Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes general rather than specifically moral assessment).

---

## 3. Rights and Duties and Justice

### Definition

This subdomain encompasses human understanding of entitlements, obligations, and fair treatment within social relationships and institutional arrangements. It captures how humans conceptualize what individuals owe to each other, what they are entitled to receive, and what constitutes fair distribution of benefits, burdens, and treatment.

### Justification

Concepts of rights, duties, and justice are fundamental to human social organization and cooperation. Humans naturally understand reciprocal obligations and entitlements that structure social relationships. This subdomain is essential because it represents the structural dimension of morality that defines the framework of mutual obligations and entitlements that enable fair and cooperative social life.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Individual rights** (human rights, civil rights, privacy, autonomy, freedom, dignity)
- **Social duties** (obligations to others, civic duties, role responsibilities, care obligations)
- **Distributive justice** (fair allocation, equality, equity, merit-based distribution, need-based distribution)
- **Procedural justice** (fair processes, due process, equal treatment, impartial procedures)
- **Reciprocity concepts** (mutual obligation, give and take, balance, exchange, reciprocal duties)
- **Entitlement concepts** (deserving, earning, merit, need-based claims, basic entitlements)
- **Justice institutions** (legal systems, courts, arbitration, conflict resolution, enforcement)
- **Social contracts** (mutual agreements, collective obligations, social cooperation, shared responsibility)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans develop understanding of reciprocal relationships and can recognize when rights are violated or duties unfulfilled. They understand various principles of justice and can apply them to evaluate social arrangements and resolve conflicts.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Governance and Law" (which focuses on legal rather than moral obligations) and "Conventions and Ethical Norms and Principles" (which emphasizes character rather than structural obligations).

---

## 4. Moral Emotions and Responses

### Definition

This subdomain captures human understanding of the emotional dimensions of moral experience, including the feelings that accompany moral judgments, moral violations, and moral achievements. It encompasses how humans conceptualize guilt, shame, pride, indignation, empathy, and other emotions that are specifically connected to moral situations and moral agency.

### Justification

Moral emotions are fundamental to human ethical motivation and moral development. Humans naturally experience emotional responses to moral situations that motivate moral behavior and signal moral significance. This subdomain is essential because it represents the affective dimension of morality that provides the emotional energy for moral action and the internal signals that guide moral behavior.

### Exemplary Concepts

- **Self-directed moral emotions** (guilt, shame, pride, regret, moral satisfaction, self-respect)
- **Other-directed moral emotions** (anger, indignation, contempt, admiration, gratitude, forgiveness)
- **Empathetic responses** (compassion, sympathy, concern, caring, emotional resonance)
- **Moral motivation** (moral outrage, righteous anger, moral passion, ethical commitment)
- **Conscience experiences** (moral qualms, ethical unease, moral clarity, inner voice)
- **Moral repair** (remorse, repentance, apology, forgiveness, reconciliation, redemption)
- **Moral inspiration** (moral elevation, ethical admiration, moral beauty, noble emotions)
- **Moral distress** (moral injury, ethical stress, moral conflict, value conflicts)

### Cognitive Relevance

Humans understand the relationship between emotions and morality and can recognize moral emotions as signals about ethical significance. They understand how moral emotions motivate behavior and can use emotional responses to guide moral decision-making.

### Boundary Considerations

Distinguished from "Emotions and Affective States" (which focuses on general rather than specifically moral emotions) and "Moral Judgments and Evaluations" (which emphasizes cognitive rather than emotional aspects of morality).

---

## Summary

The Moral domain captures the ethical dimensions of human experience and social life that enable cooperative, fair, and meaningful social existence. These four subdomains represent the key components of human moral life: the normative frameworks that guide behavior (Conventions and Ethical Norms and Principles), the evaluative processes that assess rightness and wrongness (Moral Judgments and Evaluations), the structural obligations and entitlements that govern social relationships (Rights and Duties and Justice), and the emotional experiences that motivate and signal moral significance (Moral Emotions and Responses). Together, they represent how humans create, maintain, and navigate the ethical dimensions of individual character and social cooperation that distinguish human societies and enable moral agency and ethical flourishing.
