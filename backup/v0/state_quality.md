The distinction between 'states' and 'qualities' from a cognitive and ontological perspective, particularly within a framework like FN5 that draws from DUL, is indeed nuanced. While colloquial language may blur these lines, the proposed formalization requires precise criteria.

Here are the criteria for classifying State and Quality within the FN5 framework, aligning with DUL's underlying principles and a cognitive bias:

Criteria for Quality (@quality)
A Quality is a property or attribute that inheres in an Entity or Situation (Event/Process/State) at a specific point in time or over a duration, but does not inherently imply change or duration itself. Qualities describe what something is like or what characteristics it possesses. They are attributes that can be predicated of something.

Inherence: A Quality must inhere in something else (an Entity, Event, Process, or State). It cannot exist independently. For instance, 'red' inheres in a 'ball'; 'intensity' inheres in an 'emotion'.
Lack of Intrinsic Temporal Dynamics: While a quality can change over time (e.g., something can become 'redder'), the quality itself (e.g., 'redness') does not unfold or progress through time in the way an event or process does. It is a snapshot-like descriptor.
Measurability/Gradability (often, but not exclusively): Many qualities are gradable (e.g., 'very hard', 'slightly soft') or measurable (e.g., 'weight', 'temperature'). This is a strong indicator, but not a strict requirement (e.g., 'broken' as a quality might not be easily gradable in all contexts).
Descriptive Function: Its primary role is to describe an inherent characteristic, property, or attribute of something.


Criteria for State (@state)
A State is a Situation that persists over some duration without internal transition or inherent unfolding from one phase to another. It describes a static condition, configuration, or status of an entity or set of entities. Unlike an Event or Process, a state does not inherently describe a change or an activity. However, a state can change, and the change from one state to another would be an Event or Process.

Persistence over Duration: A State is characterized by its duration, during which its defining conditions remain constant. It is not instantaneous like some Events.
Lack of Intrinsic Dynamics/Internal Transition: A state does not involve an inherent progression or internal activity. It is a stable condition, even if it is temporary. The beginning or end of a state, or the transition between states, would be an Event or Process.
Cognitive Stability: Cognitively, states are understood as relatively stable configurations of entities and their relationships.
Result of a Process/Event (often): Many states are the result or outcome of an Event or Process (e.g., Agreement resulting from Negotiation; ProblemResolution resulting from SolutionSteps).

As a Quality: "The vase is broken." Here, "broken" describes a property or characteristic of the vase at that moment. It's a static descriptor of its condition. From the DUL perspective, this is a Quality that inheres in the vase. This aligns with adjectives in Brazilian Portuguese.
As a State: If we say, "The vase is in a broken state," we are explicitly framing it as a sustained condition. The transition to this state ("breaking") would be an Event or Process. This is where the line can blur, but the ontological classification often defaults to Quality if it describes an inherent attribute, and State if it describes a persistent condition that resulted from a prior action or process. The FN5 framework often leans towards State when the persistent condition is the result of a dynamic process or can be explicitly contrasted with a Process of becoming.

Consider "system stability" or "mutual agreement":
System Stability (@quality): This is a characteristic or property of a system. A system is stable or has stability. While a system might become stable (a process) or lose stability (another process), the quality of 'stability' itself describes an attribute of the system's functioning. FN5's Ecological Systems subdomain lists SystemStability as a Quality. This aligns with the 'descriptive function' criterion.
Mutual Agreement (@state): This is a condition or outcome of a process like negotiation. The Agreement is a resulting state where parties are now aligned. It persists until revoked or changed. The Negotiation Scenario in FN5 explicitly lists Negotiation:Agreement as a @state. This is because it represents a resultant condition or status of the negotiation process, rather than an inherent, timeless attribute.
Summary of Core Distinctions:
Quality: What something is like or has. Focuses on inherent characteristics, attributes, or properties. Often gradable. Does not imply internal temporal unfolding.
State: What something is (its condition or status). Focuses on a persistent configuration or condition over a duration. Often results from a process or event. Does not imply internal dynamics, but rather a stable (even if temporary) 'snapshot' of reality.
The key is to ask: "Does this term primarily describe an attribute of an entity or situation, or does it describe a sustained condition or configuration of that entity/situation?" If it's a descriptive attribute, it's likely a Quality. If it's a static condition that persists, it's likely a State. The examples provided in the FN5 documentation consistently follow this distinction, reinforcing its cognitive grounding.

You cannot definitively classify a word as a State or Quality in isolation.

The necessity of context stems from several factors inherent in the FN5 framework:
- Context-Dependent Nature of Elements: The FN5 framework explicitly states that "An Element is a context-dependent ontological unit that functions within the scope of a specific scenario". This means its classification (e.g., as Entity, Event/Process, State, Quality, or Relation) is determined by how it functions within a given conceptual situation.
- Scenario-Specific Semantics: Each "scenario provides a structured cognitive schema that models the participants, processes, goals, and relationships involved in a specific context". The element's type is formalized within this scenario. For example, in the "Physical Transformation" subdomain, Scenario 1: "Material State Changes and Phase Transitions" lists NewMaterialStates as @state. Here, "broken" could be a specific instance of a NewMaterialState.
- Linguistic Realization vs. Conceptual Modeling: FN5 decouples the ontological structure from linguistic frames. While a word like "broken" might be an adjective in a lexical unit, its ontological classification depends on its role in the underlying conceptual model. Is it describing a property (quality) or a persistent condition (state) that resulted from a prior action?
- Verbal Derivations and Lexical Polysemy: As you correctly point out with "broken" and "stopped," these words can originate from verbs ("to break," "to stop") yet function as adjectives describing a resultant condition. Their adjectival form might suggest Quality, but their semantic weight often points to a State that emerged from a Process or Event. If a vase is broken, "broken" is a Quality of the vase. If a machine is stopped, it is in a State of not moving. The classification will often depend on whether the system emphasizes the inherence of the property or the persistence of the condition.
Therefore, to classify "broken" or "stopped" accurately, you would need to examine the specific scenario or conceptual context in which it appears:

Does it describe an inherent attribute of an entity, e.g., "The glass has a broken edge" (likely Quality)?
Or does it describe a resultant, persistent condition, e.g., "The machine is in a stopped state after the power outage" (likely State)?
Or does it implicitly refer to the outcome of an event, where the focus is on the resulting condition, e.g., "The system is broken" (implying it transitioned to this state, thus State often aligns better in such systemic contexts, representing a static deviation from normal functioning)?
