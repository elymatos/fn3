In the FN5 framework, frames in the lexical dimension are categorized according to their **semantic function**, **conceptual grounding**, and **linguistic realization**. The following types are not mutually exclusive—frames may belong to more than one category. However, each type represents a **dominant cognitive or grammatical perspective** useful for structuring the lexicon and supporting frame creation.

---

## 1. **Situational Frames**

**Definition**: Situational frames represent **events, actions, and interactions** that involve change, dynamics, temporal progression, and participant roles. These frames correspond to prototypical human experiences—both physical and social—and are central to Frame Semantics and FrameNet tradition. They typically structure verbs and deverbal nouns, often defining who does what to whom, under what conditions, and with what outcomes.

**Examples**: `Buy`, `Argue`, `Travel`, `Negotiate`, `Rescue`, `Teach`.

---

## 2. **State Frames**

**Definition**: State frames represent **static configurations or persistent conditions** of entities or systems. Unlike events, states do not unfold over time, but rather hold a condition constant over a duration. These frames often result from previous events (e.g., “agreement” after negotiation) and may define status, legal condition, possession, or roles. They are critical for modeling outcomes and background conditions in scenario modeling.

**Examples**: `Be_in_possession`, `Be_in_prison`, `Agreement`, `Is_open`, `Is_employed`.

---

## 3. **Quality Frames**

**Definition**: Quality frames describe **inherent properties or attributes** of entities or situations. They do not imply persistence, duration, or temporal dynamics. Instead, they function as **descriptors**, often realized as adjectives or dimensional nouns. From an ontological perspective, they inhere in something else (e.g., color in an object), and from a cognitive standpoint, they allow fine-grained gradability or comparison.

**Examples**: `Color`, `Weight`, `Stability`, `Softness`, `Strength`, `Brightness`.

---

## 4. **Entity Frames**

**Definition**: Entity frames categorize **classes of entities** in the world, including physical objects, living beings, social constructs, mental artifacts, and institutional actors. These frames define what something is, not what it does. They often reflect taxonomic structures, enabling inheritance and linking with the ontological dimension. Lexically, they are expressed through common nouns and sometimes proper nouns.

**Examples**: `Tool`, `Person`, `Animal`, `Institution`, `Vehicle`, `Building`.

---

## 5. **Relational Frames**

**Definition**: Relational frames encode **semantic relationships between two or more entities**, including spatial, possessive, comparative, hierarchical, or functional relations. These frames focus not on the participants themselves, but on how they are connected. They can be realized through prepositions, relational adjectives, relational nouns, or support verbs.

**Examples**: `Part_of`, `Taller_than`, `Friend_of`, `Owned_by`, `Next_to`.

---

## 6. **Image Schema Frames**

**Definition**: Image schema frames represent **cognitively grounded schematic structures**—such as `Containment`, `Path`, or `Balance`—that emerge from embodied spatial-temporal experience. These schemas underlie much of human reasoning, especially metaphor and event representation. In FN5, this category absorbs the former “Structural Frames” to emphasize the conceptual, pre-linguistic origin of many linguistic patterns.

**Examples**: `Path`, `Containment`, `Link`, `Force`, `Center-Periphery`, `Scale`.

---

## 7. **Cognitive Frames**

**Definition**: Cognitive frames represent **mental activities, operations, and internal states** such as memory, belief, reasoning, desire, attention, and perception. These are particularly important for modeling verbs of cognition and emotion, and for capturing how agents process information or evaluate the world. They also serve as conceptual bridges between linguistic meaning and internal representations.

**Examples**: `Remember`, `Plan`, `Believe`, `Decide`, `Imagine`, `Understand`.

---

## 8. **Metalinguistic Frames**

**Definition**: Metalinguistic frames describe **language used to refer to language itself**. They involve operations such as defining, quoting, paraphrasing, translating, or discussing lexical meaning. These frames are important for grammar instruction, linguistic annotation, and discourse analysis, especially when language is reflexive or quoted.

**Examples**: `Define`, `Translate`, `Quote`, `Paraphrase`, `Synonym_of`, `Correct`.

---

## 9. **Functional Frames**

**Definition**: Functional frames capture the meanings of **grammatical or functional words** such as conjunctions, prepositions, quantifiers, and modal particles. These words structure relationships between propositions, arguments, or discourse units. Functional frames are essential for syntactic-semantic alignment and often serve compositional or scaffolding roles in meaning construction.

**Examples**: `If`, `And`, `Because`, `With`, `Before`, `During`, `Without`.

---

## 10. **Pragmatic Frames**

**Definition**: Pragmatic frames model **context-dependent, interactionally situated uses of language**, especially those involving deixis, reference, speech acts, and discourse positioning. They are crucial for capturing the illocutionary force of utterances, social roles in dialogue, and indexical expressions. FN5 adopts this category from FrameNet Brasil to support applications in discourse modeling and communicative act analysis.

**Examples**: `Here`, `Now`, `You`, `Request`, `Apologize`, `Invite`, `Turn-taking`.
